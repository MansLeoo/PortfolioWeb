Drone Simulator

Drone simulator designed for flight training and survivor search in post-natural disaster environments.

IPAL: International Research Laboratory on Artificial Intelligence

IPAL is a French-Singaporean research laboratory bringing together the CNRS, the National University of Singapore, and A*STAR. Based in Singapore, this laboratory focuses on Artificial Intelligence, Human-Computer Interaction, and Virtual Reality.

Context and objectives

This project was carried out as part of my Master's degree in collaboration with the IPAL laboratory in Singapore. The main objective was to improve a drone flight simulator initially developed by the laboratory.

The simulator aims to train drone pilots to search for survivors in an urban environment devastated by a natural disaster. Furthermore, this project serves as a research platform to study the impact of User Interfaces (HCI) on the cognitive load of pilots in critical situations.

The developed application

The user is immersed in a rescue scenario where they must pilot a drone to locate victims within a time limit. The challenge lies in navigating through a dense and unstable environment, where the slightest crash must be avoided.

To assist them, the pilot has a customizable graphical interface allowing them to modulate the amount of information displayed, such as activating a map indicating the areas already explored. This feature is at the core of the study on cognitive load conducted by IPAL.

My contributions

During this project, I developed several key features to improve the realism and usefulness of the simulator:

1. Redesign of the collision system

I replaced the simple "Crash" text message (used in the first prototype) with a realistic physics system: upon impact with the environment, the drone truly crashes, interrupting its flight. This forces the pilot to exercise greater precision.

2. Dynamic victim generation

Initially fixed, the body positions are now randomly generated from a spawn point system. I also implemented an interface to configure the number of victims to find per session, and I varied the textures of their clothing to avoid visual monotony.

3. Enrichment of the audio and visual environment

I added many details to the ruined dwellings (furniture, debris, decorative elements). To enhance immersion, I reworked the sound environment by integrating spatialized 3D sounds (such as alarm sirens) and sudden random events (distant explosions).
